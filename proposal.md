# Youth AI Engineering Club — Founding Proposal
**A community-based STEM club for high school students**
*Draft for founding families — May 2026*

---

## What Is This?

A small, independent AI Engineering club co-founded by a few families. It operates as a community organization, open to students from any school.

The club runs on three verbs: **learn, practice, share.**

- **Learn** — study AI engineering concepts through real problems, not textbook exercises
- **Practice** — apply those concepts hands-on across real domains (competitions, robotics, research)
- **Share** — put your work in front of people outside the club: a video, a demo, a writeup
- **Build** *(good to have)* — when ready, ship something finished and shareable

The learn → practice → share loop applies across every domain the club touches — whether that's competitive CS, robotics, research, or something members bring in later.

## The Model: Learn Patterns, Practice on Domains

The club teaches a core set of AI engineering patterns — the techniques practitioners use today. Members then practice those patterns on real domains they already care about.

```
      AI Engineering Patterns
                ↓
  ┌─────────────────────────────┐
  │   Learn → Practice → Share  │  ← same loop, every domain
  └─────────────────────────────┘
                ↓ applied to each domain
  ─────────────────────────────────────
  ACSL (priority) · FRC (priority) · presentations · research · ...
```

### AI Engineering Patterns (What We Learn)

| Pattern | What It Is |
|---|---|
| **Prompt Engineering** | Designing and iterating prompts to get reliable, precise outputs from language models |
| **Tool Use & API Integration** | Connecting AI to external data sources, APIs, and real-world actions |
| **Evaluation & Harness Engineering** | Writing test suites to measure AI output quality and catch regressions |
| **Spec Engineering** | Writing structured specifications that AI systems can act on consistently |
| **RAG** | Grounding AI with a knowledge base so it can reason over specific documents or data |
| **Multimodal Workflows** | Combining text, image, and audio inputs in a single AI pipeline |
| **Agentic Engineering** | Building AI workflows that plan, use tools, and take multi-step actions |
| **Computer Vision Pipelines** | Building systems that interpret images or video for detection, tracking, or classification |

### Domains (Where We Practice)

Members bring their own domains. The same patterns above apply regardless of domain.

| Domain | Priority | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Competitive CS (ACSL)** | High | Directly tied to academic competition scores and awards |
| **Robotics (FRC)** | High | Directly tied to academic competition scores and awards |
| Presentations & slide decks | Standard | Immediate everyday use case |
| Research & writing | Standard | Applicable across all school subjects |
| Travel & event planning | Standard | Practical, relatable entry point for new members |
| *Member-brought domains* | Varies | Grows as the club grows |

#### Example: Competitive CS (ACSL)

ACSL topics are predictable each year. The club's approach: apply AI engineering patterns to each topic first — build a small demo, write a spec, run an evaluation — so members understand the concept through hands-on engineering before they ever see it as a contest problem. The AI engineering practice *is* the study method.

| AI Engineering Practice | How It Preps for ACSL |
|---|---|
| Build a RAG tool that answers questions from a CS concept knowledge base | Deepens understanding of graph theory, recursion, data structures through retrieval and explanation |
| Write eval harnesses that test solutions to ACSL-style problems | Forces precise thinking about edge cases and correctness — same muscle the contest tests |
| Use prompt engineering to generate and vary practice problems | Covers more ground faster; exposes the shape of each topic |
| Spec out a solution before coding it | Builds the structured reasoning habit that separates fast solvers from slow ones |

The output isn't just a better ACSL score — it's a reusable AI engineering artifact the club can publish.

#### Example: Robotics (FRC)

FRC build season runs January–April. The club's approach: use AI engineering patterns — computer vision pipelines, spec writing, evaluation harnesses — to build the technical skills that matter most during FRC build season. Members arrive on their school teams already fluent in the tools freshmen typically learn on the job.

| AI Engineering Practice | How It Preps for FRC |
|---|---|
| Build a computer vision pipeline on sample game footage | Directly applicable to autonomous game piece detection |
| Design an agentic decision workflow on sensor input data | Maps to autonomous period logic and state-based robot control |
| Write a software spec for a robot subsystem | Builds the documentation habit FRC teams often skip and regret |
| Build an eval script to benchmark model or pipeline performance | Same thinking as robot performance testing and iteration |

The club doesn't do FRC work. Members show up to their school teams with skills most freshmen spend the first build season acquiring.

## Year 1 Plan (Grade 9 — Sep 2026 to Jun 2027)

**Who:** founding students + 2–4 friends from either school
**What:** join a competition or match together as a team, or maintain a shared project across the year; maintain a youtube channel for sure
**Where:** Local library meeting room (free) or home
**Cost:** LLM service cost, match registration fees

> **On AI Engineering and ACSL:** The club uses AI engineering techniques to help members *learn and practice* ACSL concepts more effectively — not to solve contest problems. Using AI during ACSL rounds is not permitted and not something we do.

### Calendar

| Period | Club Activity | Domain Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Sep** | Form the group; pick one pattern; start a tiny hands-on exercise same session | ACSL: registration deadline ~Oct |
| **Oct** | Ship first mini-artifact (a demo, a script, a writeup) — however small | ACSL: registration deadline ~Oct |
| **Nov – Dec** | Iterate: pick next pattern, build on or replace the mini-artifact | ACSL: rounds 1–2 (Nov, Dec) |
| **Jan – Feb** | Lighter schedule; keep one small build per month alive | ACSL: rounds 3–4 (Jan, Feb); FRC: kickoff Jan, build season begins |
| **Mar** | Minimal sessions; don't drop the project thread | ACSL: round 5 (Mar); FRC: build wraps, regionals begin |
| **Apr – May** | Resume full sessions; polish the best artifact into something shareable | FRC: regionals + championship; ACSL: All-Star (May) |
| **Jun** | Share publicly — the artifact is already done, this is just the audience | — |

### How projects work

No big-bang project at the end. Members start building in the first session — something tiny is fine. Each month they either iterate on it or swap to a new problem. By June the artifact already exists; the share is just finding an audience for work that's already done.

> **Optional — ACSL as a group domain:** If all members agree to register for ACSL together, it can be treated as a shared domain and worked into the session schedule (Oct–Mar). This is a group decision, not a default.

## The 4-Year Arc

```
Grade 9   →  Start the club (2–4 kids, informal)
              Domains: ACSL + FRC
              Ship one small project, share it publicly
              Run a first small community workshop (library or school event)

Grade 10  →  Recruit 2–4 new members with new domains
              Ship a more polished project
              Build a public track record (videos, demos, writeups)
              Run a bigger community workshop

Grade 11  →  Leadership roles in club and in their domains
              Community workshops become a regular cadence
              Club becomes something others want to join

Grade 12  →  Hand off club leadership to next generation
              College essays write themselves
```

By Grade 12, the student story is:

> *"I co-founded an AI Engineering club in Grade 9 with one other student. Each year we picked a real domain — competitive CS, robotics, eventually research — and built something in it that we could actually explain to someone outside the room. By junior year we were running workshops. I handed it off when I graduated."*

That is a genuinely unusual story. Most students join clubs. These students built one.

## Who Runs This?

The students run it. The parents support logistics (venue, registration, occasional transport).

Founding students are **co-leaders from Day 1** — not participants in a parent-run program. If they own it, they'll recruit. If it feels like a class, they won't.
